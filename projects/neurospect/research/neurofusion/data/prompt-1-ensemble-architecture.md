## Whether something like this already exists

In conventional machine learning, **heterogeneous ensembles** absolutely exist, including in financial prediction; however, they almost always mean **combining different base learners or different data views at the prediction stage**. The forecast-combination literature is long-established, and financial/stock prediction papers already combine heterogeneous models and data sources because different models tend to perform well in different periods or regimes. But that literature usually fuses **forecasts, rankings, or probabilities**, not **reasoning traces, symbolic evidence, retrieval chains, and persistent memory states**. The closest finance-native analogue to your idea is probably **TradExpert**, because it already splits financial reasoning across specialised experts for distinct information types and then synthesises them with a general expert. That said, TradExpert is still fundamentally a **Mixture of Expert LLMs**. Its experts are heterogeneous mainly in **input specialisation**, not in the deeper sense you mean by different **kinds of intelligence** such as retrieval, symbolic graph memory, gradient-boosted quant models, causal modules, or self-improving evaluation memory. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28search0Ņłéturn28search10Ņłü

The recent finance paper **TRACE** gets closer to your stated philosophy than classic ensembles do. TRACE explicitly claims a **unified methodology** that combines **symbolic priors**, **dynamic graph exploration**, and **LLM-guided decision making** inside a temporal financial knowledge graph, and it also reports that adding an XGBoost baseline yields only a modest marginal gain. ## Fusion mechanisms for the four core signals

The most practical way to fuse your four core signals is to avoid jumping directly to a final trade score and instead create a **typed intermediate reasoning state**. In that state, vector retrieval does not output ŌĆ£bullishŌĆØ or ŌĆ£bearishŌĆØ; it outputs a ranked set of evidence objects. The DSLM does not output prose; it outputs a fixed schema such as `{setup_type, market_structure_state, liquidity_event, conditions_met, conditions_unmet, confidence, invalidation}`. Quant models output calibrated probabilistic forecasts or residual signals. The knowledge graph outputs temporally valid paths, motifs, neighbour regimes, and prior trade analogues. This approach is much closer to **StructGPTŌĆÖs reading-then-reasoning**, **GraphRAG/KG2RAGŌĆÖs evidence expansion**, and **FeatLLMŌĆÖs feature-generation** than to classic late fusion. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn29search1Ņłéturn13search0Ņłéturn13search10Ņłéturn29search3Ņłü

For the **evidence aggregation layer**, **Reciprocal Rank Fusion** is attractive precisely because your signal families live on incomparable score scales. Retrieval cosine scores, KG path scores, DSLM confidences, and gradient-boosted margins should not be naively normalised and added. RRF was designed to combine rankings without relying on score calibration, and modern hybrid retrieval work continues to use it because dense and sparse systems are complementary but poorly score-aligned. In NeuroFusion-13, RRF is most natural for fusing **candidate evidence items**ŌĆödocuments, graph paths, analogue trades, and rule hitsŌĆöbefore any higher-order reasoning occurs. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn5search1Ņłéturn6search0Ņłéturn6search18Ņłü

For the **reasoning layer**, the closest off-the-shelf pattern is a **router-plus-cross-attention** design. The router should be **regime-aware** and choose which modalities deserve compute on a given market state; for example, in a news-shock regime it may weight KG/event paths and DSLM classifications more heavily, while in quiet mean-reversion regimes it may emphasise boosted-tree signals and memory analogues. Once selected, modality-specific encoders can produce tokens or slots for a shared fusion block where cross-attention operates over **retrieval evidence, structured DSLM features, quant factors, and graph paths**. That design borrows from multimodal fusion surveys and from recent heterogeneous-model systems such as **Mixture of Thoughts**, **Dynamic Ensemble Reasoning**, and **Atlas**, all of which show that static all-expert invocation is often inferior to adaptive routing and interaction. For the **symbolic governance layer**, a neuro-symbolic pattern is stronger than pure attention. TRACE is especially instructive here: it mines symbolic relational motifs, constrains the admissible reasoning paths, and then allows graph exploration and LLM-guided selection inside those constraints. A similar design would let NeuroFusion-13 use the knowledge graph not just as memory, but as a **constraint system**: for example, ŌĆ£do not long if the retrieved analogue cluster contains high-confidence failure under the current volatility regimeŌĆØ, or ŌĆ£promotion of a long setup requires either confirmation from market-structure retrieval or a matching historical subgraph patternŌĆØ. That gives you a substrate for post-trade critique, route scoring, and counterfactual replay. This idea is adjacent to **Reflexion**, **Self-Refine**, and **STaR** on the language side, and to system-level compound-AI optimisation on the orchestration side; the novelty in finance would come from binding those ideas to a durable trade-memory graph rather than an ephemeral chat history. ## What would make NeuroFusion-13 genuinely novel

The four ingredients you named are **not individually novel**. Vector retrieval over a domain corpus is standard RAG; domain-specific finance language models already exist; downstream use of LLM-generated or LLM-extracted features is an active line of work; knowledge graphs and event embeddings are already used for finance; and trading systems built from multiple expert LLMs have already been published. If NeuroFusion-13 merely places these pieces side by side and averages their outputs, reviewers will likely see it as an incremental **compound system for trading**, not as a new architecture class. What would make it more than incremental is the combination of **ontological heterogeneity**, **typed reasoning-state fusion**, **persistent self-improving memory**, and **system-level optimisation**. Ontological heterogeneity means the system is not merely using thirteen differently prompted LLMs; it is combining components with different epistemic roles: retrieval, structured semantic classification, statistical forecasting, symbolic graph reasoning, regime memory, adversarial challenge, and perhaps causal or evolutionary search. Typed reasoning-state fusion means those components meet in a shared machine-readable workspace before a decision is made. Persistent self-improving memory means the graph compounds from trades, backtests, and evaluations and changes routing or evidence selection over time. System-level optimisation means the full DAG is optimised and evaluated as a system, which is exactly the problem modern compound-AI work says matters. ## Limits, failure modes, and open questions

The main theoretical limit is that **more modalities do not automatically produce more intelligence**. Ensemble theory and forecast-combination research consistently find that gains come from **useful diversity** and error decorrelation, not from raw model count. If the extra modalities collapse onto the same latent view of the market, then adding the thirteenth signal often adds complexity, latency, and overfitting risk faster than it adds information. In that sense, the ceiling on NeuroFusion-13 is set less by ŌĆ£13ŌĆØ and more by whether the modalities truly disagree for good reasons. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17search5Ņłéturn17search20Ņłéturn17search11Ņłéturn17search14Ņłü

The main practical limit in trading is **non-stationarity**. Financial markets exhibit regime change and concept drift, so a self-improving architecture can easily become a **self-reinforcing overfitter** if it treats past successful traces as timeless. The concept-drift literature in financial time series is clear that detection, adaptation, and evaluation under drift are first-class design problems, not operational details. A persistent knowledge graph helps only if it remembers **when** a pattern worked, **under what regime**, and **how quickly it decayed**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn16search3Ņłéturn16search4Ņłéturn16search7Ņłéturn16search34Ņłü

A second practical limit is **calibration and comparability**. Your core signals are not naturally commensurate: vector retrieval outputs retrieval relevance, the DSLM outputs schema-level confidence, boosted trees output statistical class scores, and the graph emits path or motif strength. Rank-based fusion can help at the evidence stage, but once you move into decision fusion you still need careful calibration, abstention logic, and uncertainty accounting, otherwise the system will simply look sophisticated while hiding brittle score arithmetic underneath. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn5search1Ņłéturn30view2Ņłü

A third limit is **system-level optimisation difficulty**. Recent compound-AI work explicitly notes that end-to-end training is hard because component interactions are often non-differentiable and because system-level preferences do not trivially decompose into local preferences. That matters directly for NeuroFusion-13: improving the DSLM marginally may worsen the router; enriching the graph may slow the retriever; adding symbolic checks may improve precision but hurt recall; and optimising one component in isolation may degrade the whole DAG. In other words, the architectureŌĆÖs hardest problem is not ŌĆ£build thirteen expertsŌĆØ, but ŌĆ£optimise the whole workflow without losing reliabilityŌĆØ. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn30view2Ņłéturn31view1Ņłü

The final limit is operational. Compound systems are measurably harder to run, monitor, and debug than single models. BerkeleyŌĆÖs compound-AI analysis highlights the need for new tracing, data-ops, and monitoring practices, while recent systems work shows that better orchestration can materially improve runtime and energy efficiency. For a live trading stack, this means your architecture is only as credible as its **latency budget, fallback logic, trace observability, ablations, and live out-of-sample evaluation protocol**. Behaviour-driven evaluation is likely a better fit than static benchmarks, because the real question is how the system behaves under regime shifts, missing data, contradictory evidence, and post-trade regret. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn30view0Ņłéturn31view0Ņłéturn18search0Ņłü

The biggest open questions are straightforward but non-trivial. Can you define a **canonical typed reasoning state** that all modalities can write to without excessive information loss? Can you prove that graph memory improves live decisions rather than merely preserving rationalisations? Can the router learn regime-conditioned modality selection without becoming another overfit model? And, most importantly, does reasoning-level fusion deliver better **risk-adjusted out-of-sample performance** than a brutally simple late-fusion benchmark built from the same ingredients? Those questions are unanswered in the public literature I found, and they are exactly where a NeuroFusion-13 paper could make a real contribution.
