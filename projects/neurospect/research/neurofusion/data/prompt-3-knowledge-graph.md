# NeuroGraph for systematic trading

## Executive synthesis

The strongest conclusion from the literature is that NeuroGraph should not be treated as ŌĆ£a graph database containing backtestsŌĆØ. It becomes strategically valuable only if it is **temporal**, **confidence-aware**, **provenance-complete**, and **decision-coupled**. ## Temporal knowledge graphs in finance with confidence decay

The finance-specific knowledge graph literature has moved beyond static entity graphs. A 2025 survey of financial knowledge graphs classifies the field into static, temporal, hybrid, and application-specific graphs, and explicitly notes their use in market trend analysis, enterprise risk management, fraud detection, and dynamic forecasting. The same survey also points to the growing role of GNNs and LLMs in financial KG construction and analysis. In other words, the field already recognises that time-dependent, heterogeneous, evolving relationships are central in finance. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view1Ņłéturn39view3Ņłéturn39view0Ņłéturn39view2Ņłü

What is still comparatively sparse is **directly published work on confidence-decayed temporal KGs for trading systems**. The finance papers I found focus more on extracting evolving event graphs and then learning on them than on a formal reinforcement-and-decay calculus for every edge. Cheng et al. proposed a knowledge-graph-based event embedding framework for quantitative investment; FinDKG uses an LLM to generate a dynamic financial KG from news and a KGTransformer for investing; FinKario constructs an event-enhanced financial KG from research reports and reports backtest gains over baseline systems. That is strong evidence that ŌĆ£market memory as graphŌĆØ is now mainstream enough to publish on, but it also suggests that your confidence-decayed NeuroGraph would still be novel at the execution layer. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn4search0Ņłéturn23view0Ņłéturn19view5Ņłü

The best research basis for your edge-confidence logic comes from general temporal KG work rather than finance papers. TempValid models the **temporal validity of temporal rules** and explicitly introduces a time function with controlled decay rate, learnable confidence, and learnable decay coefficients. CEHis adds a **confidence estimator with history** for temporal KG reasoning and uses a Hawkes-process-style mechanism so that recent historical accuracy matters more than older accuracy. CTRIEJ goes one step further by jointly embedding **uncertainty, time, and structure** in uncertain temporal KGs. These three strands map very naturally onto a graph in which, for example, `feature predicts_outcome`, `model_version outperforms_in_regime`, or `trade used_setup` edges strengthen with successful reinforcement and fade with stale or contradictory evidence. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view0Ņłéturn21view1Ņłéturn21view0Ņłü

For NeuroGraph, the practical design implication is that you should avoid a single global forgetting curve. Relation types age differently. A relation like `model_version trained_on_dataset` should usually be immutable and non-decaying; `event near_trade` may decay quickly in practical relevance; `setup profitable_in_regime` should decay if the regime changes; `behavioural_pattern predicts_slippage` may need both decay and shock resets after market-structure breaks. The literature on temporal rule validity, history-weighted confidence, and uncertain TKG embedding supports **relation-specific and regime-specific decay**, not a uniform half-life. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view0Ņłéturn21view1Ņłéturn21view0Ņłü

On the embedding methods you named, the trade-offs are quite clear. **TTransE** is simple and easy to operationalise, but the surveys describe it as a model that effectively concatenates time with relations and note that TTransE and similar variants struggle to handle facts that undergo meaningful temporal evolution. **DE-SimplE** uses diachronic entity embeddings and is proved fully expressive for temporal KG completion, making it a stronger candidate when entities or regimes evolve smoothly over time. **TNTComplEx** extends temporal KG completion to an order-4 tensor and explicitly separates temporal and non-temporal components, which is attractive in trading because some parts of your graph are stable governance facts while others are regime-sensitive and fast-moving. For a graph mixing stable lineage with changing market structure, TNTComplEx is usually the more natural baseline; for smoothly drifting entities, DE-SimplE is very strong; TTransE is best treated as a baseline rather than a production choice. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view0Ņłéturn29view3Ņłéturn18view6Ņłéturn29view0Ņłéturn18view7Ņłü

That said, a pure embedding model is unlikely to be enough for finance, because regime changes are often abrupt, sparse, and explanation-sensitive. Recent TKG work increasingly favours interpretable or structured alternatives: TILP learns differentiable temporal logical rules and performs well especially when samples are limited, biased, or training and inference time ranges differ; T-GAP focuses on temporal displacement, path-based inference, and generalisation to unseen timestamps. Those properties are unusually relevant to trading, where regime breaks, sparse setups, and the need to explain why a strategy was selected all matter. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn30view0Ņłéturn30view1Ņłü

## Model lineage and strategy genealogy

Public evidence suggests that sophisticated quant organisations care a great deal about lineage, but they reveal little about internal implementation details. What is visible points in the same direction as your design. D. E. Shaw collaborated on Versioned HDF5 specifically because, for quantitative time-series research, it is crucial not to rely on data available at time **T2** when generating results for **T1**; their write-up explicitly calls ŌĆ£time travel through versioned data setsŌĆØ critical for accurate and reproducible results. That is exactly the anti-lookahead foundation a persistent trading graph needs. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view16Ņłü

The regulatory case against Two Sigma is the clearest public reminder that governance failures in systematic-model stacks can be economically material. In January 2025, the SEC said Two Sigma had identified vulnerabilities in certain investment models by or before March 2019 but did not address them until August 2023; during that period, an employee made unauthorised changes to more than a dozen models, affecting investment decisions. Two Sigma repaid impacted funds and accounts $165 million and agreed to pay $90 million in penalties. For NeuroGraph, that is not just a compliance anecdote; it is an argument for explicit model/version/parameter lineage as a first-class graph. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view17Ņłü

The broader ML lineage literature is already graph-shaped. AWS describes model lineage in quantitative finance as the joint problem of code lineage, data lineage, and model lineage, and argues that a graph structure is the most suitable way to represent the highly associated artefacts involved in model creation. The same piece explicitly discusses quants varying parameters, datasets, and Sharpe-ratio targets over many experiments and notes that spreadsheets stop scaling. Modern production tooling has converged on this: ML Metadata records metadata for ML workflows, MLflow links model versions back to experiments and runs, W&B visualises artefact lineage as DAGs, and OpenLineage standardises the collection of lineage events for jobs, runs, and datasets. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn36view1Ņłéturn18view9Ņłéturn18view10Ņłéturn18view11Ņłéturn19view10Ņłü

There is also an emerging research strand that explicitly uses the language of **model genealogy**. A 2024 model-provenance paper argues that existing model cards and system cards fall short at tracing genealogy and proposes a machine-readable representation of upstream and downstream model relationships; the associated Unified Model Records repository makes that relationship graph versionable and queryable. In parallel, ShadowGenes frames model genealogy as identifying a modelŌĆÖs architecture, type, and family from its computational graph. This is not yet quant-specific, but it shows that ŌĆ£model genealogy graphŌĆØ is becoming a real concept rather than a metaphor. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn38view0Ņłéturn38view1Ņłéturn37search1Ņłü

For a trading platform, the right interpretation is that **strategy genealogy** should sit beside **model genealogy**. A strategy is not merely a backtest row. The standards literature does not hand you this schema fully formed, but W3C PROV explicitly offers a domain-agnostic model of entities, activities, agents, derivations, and provenance-of-provenance that is well suited to extending into a quant-specific genealogy graph. ## GNNs over trading and market-event graphs

There is now strong evidence that GNNs can discover patterns that flat feature vectors miss in financial settings, mainly because many non-graph approaches assume independence where the market is actually networked. A survey of financial GNN methods states that non-graph approaches to stock prediction often implicitly assume stocks are independent, whereas GNN approaches represent inter-stock relations explicitly and learn on top of them. That is the central reason a graph layer can add value: information often resides in dependencies, not just in the marginal features of one instrument or one trade. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn22view1Ņłéturn21view5Ņłü

In stock prediction, this is already well documented. Matsunaga et al. tested the use of company knowledge graphs directly inside stock-prediction models with rolling-window backtests. THGNN then pushed further by building a relation graph for each trading day from historic prices, combining a transformer encoder with a heterogeneous graph attention network, and outperforming state-of-the-art baselines on both US and Chinese markets. The key point is not merely that graphs ŌĆ£workŌĆØ, but that **dynamic** relations work better than static hand-built ones when market dependencies themselves move over time. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view4Ņłéturn18view3Ņłü

The same pattern appears in more market-structure-oriented work. ŌĆ£Graph PortfolioŌĆØ uses spatio-temporal GNNs for high-frequency futures prediction, arguing that each futures contract depends not only on its own history but also on the cross-sectional behaviour of other contracts; it reports improved prediction accuracy on 49 commodity futures. ŌĆ£Trading Graph Neural NetworkŌĆØ builds a trading-network model that jointly reasons over asset features, dealer features, and relationship features and outperforms reduced-form approaches based on centrality measures. These are good analogues for a NeuroGraph layer over trades, setups, liquidity events, counterparties, or venue interactions. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27view0Ņłéturn27view1Ņłü

For a trading-event graph specifically, the important opportunity is to move from ŌĆ£snapshot featuresŌĆØ to **motifs** and **paths**. Flat vectors are usually good at monotone relationships such as ŌĆ£higher ATR means worse fillŌĆØ. A graph can additionally learn structures such as: *sweep ŌåÆ displacement ŌåÆ FVG ŌåÆ partial retrace ŌåÆ entry ŌåÆ adverse excursion only when nearby higher-timeframe order block is absent and volatility regime is high*. That kind of conditional, multi-hop structure is exactly where knowledge graphs and GNNs tend to outperform table-only modelling. The financial GNN survey repeatedly highlights the need to model heterogeneity, dynamics, and multiple data sources jointly, which is close to your proposed nodes and edges. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn22view0Ņłéturn22view4Ņłéturn21view5Ņłü

A sensible architectural choice is therefore not ŌĆ£replace your tabular pipeline with a GNNŌĆØ, but ŌĆ£use the graph to generate regime-aware, path-aware, and cluster-aware features for downstream modelsŌĆØ. NeuroGraph can supply node embeddings, relation counts, motif frequencies, edge-confidence aggregates, neighbourhood histories, and path scores into LightGBM or another tabular model. That hybrid usually fits finance better than end-to-end black-box GNN execution, because it preserves auditability and allows you to inspect which graph-derived signals add incremental predictive value. ## Counterfactual reasoning for strategy optimisation

Your example question ŌĆö ŌĆ£if this filter had been applied to the last 200 trades, Sharpe improves by 0.3ŌĆØ ŌĆö should be treated as a **counterfactual policy-evaluation** problem, not as an ordinary backtest rerun. The off-policy evaluation literature is directly relevant here. Saito and Joachims describe counterfactual estimators as tools for estimating how a new policy would have performed using data logged by a different policy, effectively giving an offline A/B test. AmazonŌĆÖs 2025 work extends that logic to two-stage recommender systems and evaluates new candidate generators through off-policy evaluation rather than naive offline metrics. A trading filter or strategy selector is structurally the same kind of object. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view7Ņłéturn19view8Ņłü

That leads to a concrete design rule for NeuroGraph: you should log not only executed trades, but also **candidate trades that were considered and rejected**, together with the state, available actions, ranking score, gating rules, and, if possible, the propensity or selection probability under the live policy. Without those rejected candidates, most ŌĆ£what if we had used filter X?ŌĆØ claims collapse into biased historical replay. With them, you can estimate the effect of changing the filter, ranking policy, or model-routing rule under a contextual-bandit or policy-evaluation framework. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view7Ņłéturn19view8Ņłü

The graph layer helps because it stores the causal neighbourhood of each decision. A trade node can connect to the exact market-event nodes, regime node, model version, feature snapshot, execution context, and subsequent outcome path that were available at decision time. Counterfactual KG reasoning research like COULDD is not finance-specific, but it is conceptually useful: it treats counterfactual reasoning as reasoning over a graph under hypothetical edge additions or modifications. In trading terms, that means you can ask not only ŌĆ£what if this trade were filtered out?ŌĆØ but also ŌĆ£what if this event had been interpreted as a liquidity sweep rather than noise?ŌĆØ or ŌĆ£what if the regime router had promoted model B instead of model A?ŌĆØ. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view6Ņłü

There is, however, a critical boundary. Off-policy evaluation is strongest when policy changes do **not** strongly alter the environment. If your proposed filter simply decides whether to take a signal, OPE can be effective. If your proposed change materially affects execution, impact, or inventory path, then you also need a market simulator. Emerging finance work on causal market simulators argues exactly this: synthetic or causal simulators are useful for stress testing and generating plausible counterfactual market trajectories when static historical replay is insufficient. In practice, the cleanest NeuroGraph stack is: OPE for signal-selection counterfactuals, and simulation for execution-impact counterfactuals. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11search2Ņłéturn10search20Ņłü

## What makes a knowledge graph a real moat

A graph becomes a moat when it accumulates **non-commoditised structure** rather than just non-commoditised rows. NetflixŌĆÖs knowledge-graph write-up is particularly instructive: it stresses ontology-driven modelling, semantic connectivity, rapid evolution as new entity types appear, embedded provenance, timestamps, and confidence scores on triples, while also feeding graph-derived embeddings into ML for personalisation, search, prediction, and recommendations. That is far closer to a moat than a warehouse export, because it compounds meaning and trust, not just storage volume. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view0Ņłü

Amazon and Spotify show complementary transfer lessons. AmazonŌĆÖs recommendation research uses a commonsense product graph to connect products to human contexts such as functions, audiences, and locations; Spotify productionised a two-tower HGNN system that overcame severe cold start by borrowing signal from adjacent content domains and reported large lifts in audiobook starts and streaming. The transferable lesson for trading is that a graph is most valuable when it lets one sparse domain borrow strength from adjacent structure. A rare trading setup in a new market regime can inherit evidence from similar event motifs, related instruments, neighbouring regimes, or ancestor strategies rather than starting from zero. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view14Ņłéturn19view3Ņłéturn18view13Ņłü

Recommendation research also offers an important warning. KG4RecEval found that adding a knowledge graph does **not** automatically improve recommendation accuracy; in some settings, removing or distorting the KG did not reduce performance. The implication for NeuroGraph is blunt: a graph is not a moat merely because it is a graph. If the graph is poorly curated, weakly coupled to decisions, or contains low-value relations, it can become expensive ceremony. The moat comes from **high-signal ontology design, rigorous provenance, and feedback loops that improve decision quality over time**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłü

Put differently, the durable edge in quant is not ŌĆ£we stored trades in Neptune/Neo4j/JanusGraphŌĆØ. It is that your graph can answer questions competitors cannot answer reliably: *which model lineage branch works in which regime*, *which patterns recur across markets but decay under certain liquidity states*, *which filters are causally useful rather than merely correlated*, *which strategy variant was live when this anomaly happened*, and *which semantic event motifs transfer to cold-start setups*. Public lineage systems, provenance standards, and recommendation KGs all support that interpretation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn36view1Ņłéturn18view8Ņłéturn19view10Ņłéturn34view0Ņłü

## Recommended architecture for NeuroGraph

The most defensible design is a **dual-layer graph**. The first layer is a **provenance graph**: datasets, feature snapshots, event detectors, experiments, model versions, prompts or templates, deployments, and post-mortems linked using PROV-like relations such as `used`, `wasGeneratedBy`, `derivedFrom`, and `wasAssociatedWith`. The second layer is a **market-and-strategy graph**: trades, FVGs, sweeps, order blocks, structure shifts, regime states, behavioural patterns, instruments, venues, and execution outcomes. Keeping both layers in one queryable system is what lets strategy genealogy meet market memory. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view8Ņłéturn36view0Ņłéturn19view10Ņłü

Every edge in the market-and-strategy layer should carry at least five attributes: `valid_time`, `recorded_time`, `confidence`, `evidence_count`, and `source`. You want both **valid time** and **recorded time** because anti-lookahead requires knowing when a fact became true and when your system actually knew it. Confidence should not be a raw success ratio; it should be updated with Bayesian or ranking-based evidence and then decayed with a relation-specific half-life, inspired by TempValid, CEHis, and uncertain-TKG work. In practice, that means something like a relation-specific log-odds update with a decay term tied to regime and relation type, rather than a na├»ve rolling average. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view16Ņłéturn19view0Ņłéturn21view1Ņłéturn21view0Ņłü

For representation learning, I would use three families of graph-native features side by side. First, **tensor or diachronic embeddings** such as TNTComplEx or DE-SimplE for dense similarity and link prediction. Secondly, **interpretable rule learners** such as TILP or T-GAP for ŌĆ£why this pattern matters nowŌĆØ explanations and better behaviour under sparse or shifted time ranges. Thirdly, **GNN-derived motif and neighbourhood features** over the event/trade/regime graph, exported into your tabular alpha models. That combination gives you expressive memory, interpretability, and strong downstream compatibility. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view6Ņłéturn18view7Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn30view1Ņłéturn18view3Ņłéturn27view0Ņłü

For regime-conditional model selection, store performance edges such as `model_version outperforms_in_regime regime_state` as **time-scoped, confidence-weighted, and benchmark-relative** rather than absolute. A model should not simply accrue confidence forever because one quiet volatility cluster favoured it in the past. The confidence update should be benchmarked against a stable challenger set and decay when the regime node drifts or when OPE shows the routing advantage has disappeared. That same logic should apply to feature-efficacy edges like `feature predicts_outcome` and to behavioural edges like `behavioural_pattern predicts_slippage`. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view0Ņłéturn21view1Ņłéturn36view1Ņłü

For strategy optimisation, build a **counterfactual workbench** on top of the graph. Each decision should persist the candidate set, policy score, action taken, action rejected, and eventual outcome node. Graph queries can then supply the logged data needed for off-policy evaluation of new filters, routers, or candidate generators. If you later ask ŌĆ£would adding this sweep-quality filter have improved the last 200 trades?ŌĆØ, the answer should come from logged decision context plus OPE, not from a fresh retrospective rewrite of history. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view7Ņłéturn19view8Ņłü

Finally, expose the graph in two consumption modes. Your researchers will need graph-native traversal and motif mining, but many quant workflows still live in tabular pipelines. Netflix explicitly notes that graph data often needs to be materialised into relational views for analysts and data scientists; SpotifyŌĆÖs production work similarly balances graph richness with a low-latency serving architecture. NeuroGraph should therefore publish both graph queries and reproducible relational feature views. That dual interface is one of the simplest ways to make the graph operational rather than ornamental. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view0Ņłéturn19view3Ņłü

## Open questions and limitations

The literature is now rich enough to justify NeuroGraph, but it is not yet mature enough to offer a single accepted design for **confidence-decayed temporal trading graphs**. Most of the confidence-decay machinery still comes from general temporal KG reasoning rather than published trading systems, so some of your implementation choices will remain original engineering rather than direct replication of finance papers. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view0Ņłéturn21view1Ņłéturn21view0Ņłéturn23view0Ņłéturn19view5Ņłü

Public knowledge about how top quant funds implement strategy genealogy is limited. What is visible is the importance of time-travelled data, governance, reproducibility, and model controls; the internal graph schemas and ranking logic of firms like Renaissance, Citadel, or D. E. Shaw remain proprietary.
