# NeuroFusion-13 technical design for systematic trading

## Design premise

NeuroFusion-13 should not be built as thirteen equal-weight scores feeding a final vote. The attached research converges on a different interpretation: a finance-specific compound neuro-symbolic system in which heterogeneous modules write to a typed intermediate reasoning state, persistent memory is bitemporal and active, and a regime-aware meta-layer decides when to invoke, trust, suppress, or challenge each expert. That is the difference between a decorative ensemble and a trading operating system. [R1][R3][R5]

For systematic trading, that immediately implies a scope boundary. The full 13-signal stack is not a sensible microsecond HFT brain; it is far better suited to intraday, event-driven, medium-frequency, portfolio-construction, and research-control workflows. If you need sub-millisecond execution, NeuroFusion should parameterise or supervise simpler execution algos rather than decide every order in real time. The research corpus repeatedly flags latency, complexity, monitoring and overfitting risk as first-class design constraints, which is why the architecture below is intentionally tiered into hot, warm and cold paths rather than forcing every signal into every decision. [R1][R2][R5]

Internal attachment mapping used below: [R1] *NeuroFusion-13 and the state of heterogeneous reasoning architectures*; [R2] *DSLM Features for a SIHRE Quant Stack*; [R3] *NeuroGraph for systematic trading*; [R4] *Novel Quant Trading Technology Beyond Conventional Backtesting*; [R5] *Deep research for extending SIHRE into a genuinely novel quant architecture*.

The separate discretionary AI coaching feature should remain out of the NeuroFusion execution DAG. It may share deployment controls, lineage, monitoring and security infrastructure, but it should not write features into the live alpha loop unless it is separately reframed, legally reviewed and revalidated as a trading signal. That separation matters because EU guidance treats certain employment and worker-management AI uses as high-risk, and UK ICO guidance says AI involving systematic and extensive evaluation of personal aspects with legal or similarly significant effects can trigger data protection impact assessment duties; if the coaching system is used to evaluate or monitor traders as workers, its legal and governance surface becomes meaningfully different from a pure market-prediction stack. citeturn6view4turn10view0turn6view3turn5view5

## Signal-by-signal optimisation

The cleanest operating model is to define three runtime tiers. The **hot path** is the live execution path and should stay mostly deterministic and cheap, typically within a sub-5 ms budget for intraday systematic trading. The **warm path** can enrich candidate trades when additional intelligence is worth 5–50 ms of delay. The **cold path** is seconds to days and should contain discovery, falsification, simulation, search and collaboration. Any signal that cannot be replayed deterministically from a frozen as-of snapshot should not be admitted to the hot path. [R1][R2][R4][R5]

**Signal 1 — VectorDB retrieval.** **Latency:** warm path by default; target under 10 ms for top-*k* retrieval from an in-memory local ANN index, and under 25 ms if cross-shard or cross-asset analogue search is needed. **Anti-lookahead:** every chunk must carry `valid_time`, `recorded_time`, source release timestamp and vendor arrival timestamp; the retrieval index itself must be snapshotted by backtest date so that historical replay queries the archived index rather than today’s rebuilt index. **Pipeline integration:** index not just text, but event primitives, historical trade cohorts, failure motifs, prompt artefacts and post-mortems keyed to shared ontology IDs. **Backtest-ability:** freeze embedding model, chunking, metadata filters, reranker and retrieval hyperparameters; replay should return the same evidence object IDs. **Regulatory:** control vendor entitlements and provenance; if the retrieval service or index is outsourced, the firm still retains responsibility for compliance, and DORA-style ICT third-party risk controls become relevant for in-scope EU financial entities. **Cost at scale:** medium at inference, but memory-heavy; embedding refreshes and index rebuilds can become expensive, so most cost should be pushed into batch jobs and incremental updates rather than live recomputation. [R1][R2][R3][R4] citeturn5view0turn12view0turn12view1

**Signal 2 — Domain-specific LLM.** **Latency:** warm path or precomputed path, not the core execution loop; target roughly 20–150 ms for local, quantised inference with tightly bounded prompts, and move it to bar-close or event-ingest precomputation when latency tolerance is lower than that. **Anti-lookahead:** use dated weight snapshots, frozen prompts, frozen decoding parameters, frozen tool access and point-in-time context only; run contamination diagnostics, maintain temporal partitions between model training and evaluation, and use entity anonymisation in backtests where memorisation risk is material. **Pipeline integration:** never emit prose into the trading engine; emit typed features such as setup flags, scalar scores, uncertainty metrics, invalidation conditions and evidence references. **Backtest-ability:** cache completions or enforce deterministic decoding with prompt/model/version hashes, retrieval snapshot hashes and random seeds. **Regulatory:** treat the DSLM as a governed model, not a clever helper; if bank-affiliated, updated US banking guidance still emphasises development, validation, monitoring and governance, while also explicitly noting that generative and agentic AI are outside the scope of that guidance, which argues for stricter internal controls rather than looser ones. **Cost at scale:** high GPU cost relative to value unless conditionally invoked, which is why the orchestrator should call it only for candidate trades, event windows, or disagreement cases. [R1][R2][R4] citeturn6view1turn6view2

**Signal 3 — Quant ML models.** **Latency:** hot path; target 50 microseconds to 5 ms end-to-end depending on feature hydration, model family and serving stack. **Anti-lookahead:** point-in-time feature store, bitemporal corporate actions and revisions, purged and embargoed labels where horizons overlap, and walk-forward or CPCV-style validation only. **Pipeline integration:** this remains the primary execution scorer and should consume market, microstructure, execution, regime, semantic and graph-derived features through one canonical feature service. **Backtest-ability:** strongest of all signals; models should be fully reproducible from frozen training windows and feature snapshots. **Regulatory:** for in-scope EU and US securities workflows, this layer must sit behind deterministic pre-trade controls, thresholding, controlled deployment, kill-switches and audit logs rather than “best effort” software hygiene. **Cost at scale:** low-to-medium; training can be non-trivial, but inference is cheap enough to sit on every decision. [R2][R4] citeturn11view0turn6view6turn8view0

**Signal 4 — Persistent knowledge graph.** **Latency:** two-speed design; shallow key-based lookups or precomputed path features can be hot or warm path under roughly 2–20 ms, while deep traversal, GNN passes and rule mining should be cold path. **Anti-lookahead:** make the graph bitemporal, with both `valid_time` and `recorded_time`; every node and edge needs provenance, source, confidence, evidence count and version lineage. **Pipeline integration:** this is the canonical memory layer connecting market events, trades, features, prompts, model versions, backtests, deployments, incidents and outcomes. **Backtest-ability:** use graph snapshots or time-travel queries so that backtests ask the graph what the system knew then, not what the graph knows now. **Regulatory:** this is the natural place to satisfy change provenance, model lineage, issue tracking and post-mortem traceability; the public Two Sigma enforcement action is a reminder that weak model governance and unauthorised changes can become very expensive very quickly. **Cost at scale:** medium storage and ingestion cost, with potentially high graph-compute cost if you insist on full online traversal or online GNNs; the right optimisation is precompute first, query second. [R3][R4] citeturn6view0turn5view0turn6view5

**Signal 5 — Causal discovery.** **Latency:** cold path for discovery and refutation; only the resulting causal-stability score or veto graph should reach the live path, ideally as a 1–10 ms lookup from a precomputed regime-local causal object. **Anti-lookahead:** discover structure only on historical windows available at the decision date, scoped by instrument, horizon, event family and regime; never learn one timeless “market DAG”. **Pipeline integration:** consume the graph ontology, regime labels and point-in-time features; write back causal edges, refutation results, forbidden paths and stability scores. **Backtest-ability:** run causal rediscovery in rolling or expanding windows so that the live simulation sees only the graphs that would have existed at that point. **Regulatory:** useful as a challenge function and documentation layer, but do not overclaim “causal” to clients or governance committees unless the identifying assumptions are explicit and tested. **Cost at scale:** high offline, low online once reduced to regime-local objects and scores. [R5]

**Signal 6 — Conformal prediction.** **Latency:** hot path wrapper; target around 0.1–2 ms on top of the base predictive model once residual stores and rolling calibration windows are in memory. **Anti-lookahead:** calibration must be sequential and strictly after model fitting, with rolling or online recalibration under drift; never calibrate on future residuals. **Pipeline integration:** conformal should wrap not only the quant model but any predictive expert that emits a score, turning heterogeneous outputs into a common uncertainty language. **Backtest-ability:** excellent, provided calibration windows and conformity scores are frozen by date and regime. **Regulatory:** there is no special “conformal rule”, but it is ideal for prudent risk controls because it lets you tie sizing, abstention and kill-switch logic to observable coverage health instead of vague confidence claims. **Cost at scale:** low. [R5]

**Signal 7 — Multi-agent adversarial verification.** **Latency:** warm or cold path only; target roughly 100–500 ms when invoked synchronously, and prefer asynchronous or pre-trade challenge queues for slower strategies. **Anti-lookahead:** debating agents must see only the same frozen reasoning state and evidence available at the decision timestamp. **Pipeline integration:** do not let agents produce persuasive prose as the final output; require them to produce executable checks, contradictions, missing-evidence flags, causal objections, synthetic stress requests and verifier scores. **Backtest-ability:** replay using cached agent traces, prompt hashes and evidence IDs. **Regulatory:** position this as an independent challenge function, not as a replacement for formal surveillance; MiFID RTS 6 requires automated surveillance, annual review of that surveillance and an independent risk-control capability for real-time monitoring, which means free-form debate cannot be your only line of defence. **Cost at scale:** high, which is why it should be threshold-triggered rather than universal. [R5] citeturn11view0

**Signal 8 — Synthetic scenario generation.** **Latency:** cold path only; train nightly, weekly or by research demand, never inline with order submission. **Anti-lookahead:** generators must be trained only on data available by cutoff date, and synthetic outputs must be clearly tagged as synthetic with lineage to training windows and conditioning controls. **Pipeline integration:** use it primarily for stress testing, coverage failure analysis, tail scenario generation and world-model conditioning, not for padding in-sample backtests. **Backtest-ability:** keep synthetic scenarios outside the official realised OOS statistics; they belong in robustness dashboards and challenge harnesses, not in headline PnL claims. **Regulatory:** useful for resilience and stress testing, especially because DORA and MiFID both point towards formal testing and stress practices, but synthetic evidence must never be represented as observed market performance. **Cost at scale:** very high for training and targeted generation, moderate for playback once scenarios are stored. [R4][R5] citeturn6view5turn11view0

**Signal 9 — Active learning.** **Latency:** nearline only; daily to weekly is appropriate. **Anti-lookahead:** the selector may choose the next research action only from information that existed at the time of selection, which means the research queue itself must be event-sourced and time-stamped. **Pipeline integration:** read conformal miscoverage, verifier disagreement, cluster sparsity, failure motifs and backtest-budget usage; write prioritised research actions rather than trade scores. **Backtest-ability:** feasible but only through nested simulation of the research process, because active learning changes which experiments are run and therefore what becomes known. **Regulatory:** the search process itself is part of model governance; preserving it helps defend against data-snooping and undocumented research drift. **Cost at scale:** medium computational cost but potentially high organisational leverage, because it decides where scarce research attention goes. [R4][R5]

**Signal 10 — Federated learning.** **Latency:** offline rounds only; never place it in the execution loop. **Anti-lookahead:** each client or desk update must be time-bounded, and rounds must be replayable as they would have occurred then, not with later local data mixed in. **Pipeline integration:** do not federate the full alpha model by default; federate latent representations, router statistics or small specialist submodels, and keep client-specific local heads. **Backtest-ability:** simulate asynchronous rounds and client drift explicitly, otherwise the backtest will overstate synchronisation benefits. **Regulatory:** privacy, confidentiality, data localisation, competition and third-party risk all matter here; if personal or worker data ever enters the training stream, data-minimisation and impact-assessment duties become stronger. **Cost at scale:** medium-to-high because the real cost is communication, cryptography, orchestration and privacy defence, not just gradient maths. [R5] citeturn6view3turn12view0turn12view2

**Signal 11 — Contrastive regime discovery.** **Latency:** cold path for training, hot path for assignment; target 0.1–2 ms for live regime embedding lookup or classifier inference. **Anti-lookahead:** a subtle but critical constraint is to forbid future information leaking through window construction or augmentations; finance-native positive pairs should be based on same mechanism or event structure, not on window overlap that sneaks in future state. **Pipeline integration:** output a regime embedding or posterior that conditions the quant model, conformal wrapper, causal layer, retrieval filters and orchestrator. **Backtest-ability:** very good in walk-forward mode, provided the embedding model is frozen by retrain date and assignment uses only past context. **Regulatory:** treated as a material model if it changes routing or sizing; document interpretability limits and monitor drift in cluster quality. **Cost at scale:** medium for training, low for inference. [R5]

**Signal 12 — Program synthesis.** **Latency:** cold path for search, very fast at runtime once accepted; a compiled rule or DSL programme should execute well under 1 ms if admitted to live trading. **Anti-lookahead:** the typed DSL must be constrained so that no generated programme can access non-point-in-time fields, future revisions, or unlogged external data; every attempted strategy must enter the registry, not just the winners. **Pipeline integration:** programme synthesis should produce candidate rules, feature generators or strategy variants that are sent through the same backtest, cost, slippage and deployment pipeline as human-written candidates. **Backtest-ability:** central, but only if the entire search tree is logged, otherwise you lose the information needed to control data-mining risk. **Regulatory:** every accepted programme is still an algorithmic trading strategy that must pass formal testing, controlled deployment and change-approval standards rather than sneaking in as “research output”. **Cost at scale:** high search cost, low serving cost. [R4][R5] citeturn11view0

**Signal 13 — Meta-orchestrator.** **Latency:** hot path if lightweight; target 0.1–2 ms using a contextual bandit, GBDT router or similarly auditable policy rather than deep RL on day one. **Anti-lookahead:** the router may use only contemporaneous or lagged health features, such as regime, freshness, recent live coverage health, disagreement, latency cost and strategy class; never let it train on post-decision outcomes that were not available at the route date. **Pipeline integration:** read all signal outputs and health metrics, decide which experts to invoke, how much to trust them and when to abstain, and write every routing decision and its reasons back to the graph. **Backtest-ability:** requires nested walk-forward replay because routing itself is a learned policy. **Regulatory:** treat it as a primary control algorithm in its own right, because it decides which evidence is even allowed to matter; for bank-affiliated entities it deserves governance at least as strong as conventional model-risk practice, and arguably stronger because agentic and generative components remain a moving target in supervisory guidance. **Cost at scale:** medium, but with enormous leverage because it controls the invocation cost of everything else. [R1][R5] citeturn6view1turn6view2

## Synergies, redundancies and dependencies

The highest-value synergies are the ones where one signal grounds, calibrates or falsifies another. VectorDB retrieval plus the DSLM turns corpus memory into typed semantic features rather than fuzzy narrative. Retrieval plus the knowledge graph turns that into a provenance-aware memory fabric, where the same event object can be retrieved semantically, traversed relationally and linked to realised outcomes. Quant ML plus conformal prediction gives you the production forecaster with a common uncertainty dialect. Causal discovery plus conformal prediction gives you mechanism-aware predictions with live uncertainty discipline. Contrastive regime discovery plus the meta-orchestrator yields regime-adaptive expert weighting. Synthetic scenario generation plus adversarial verification creates targeted failure generation instead of decorative augmentation. Program synthesis plus causal, conformal and verifier constraints upgrades “alpha mining” into constrained hypothesis generation. [R1][R2][R3][R4][R5]

The most important redundancy is structural, not intellectual. VectorDB retrieval and the knowledge graph should share one memory substrate — call it **NeuroMemory** — even if they remain separate signals, because they are two access patterns over the same temporal evidence fabric. Likewise, the DSLM and multi-agent verifier can share the same model-serving infrastructure, but they should remain separately versioned and independently prompted so that the challenger is not merely a paraphrase of the advocate. Active learning is partly redundant with the meta-orchestrator on the research plane, so I would merge active-learning queueing into the orchestrator’s research scheduler rather than running it as a completely separate product surface. Federated learning is the clearest optional signal: strategic if you truly have decentralised data silos, marginal if you do not. [R2][R3][R5]

The dependency graph is not flat. The signals with the most independent value are Quant ML, the knowledge graph, and contrastive regime discovery, because they can be built directly from point-in-time market, event and execution data. VectorDB retrieval depends on the shared ontology and archived corpora. The DSLM depends on retrieval and graph-grounded context if you want it to be more than a generic classifier. Causal discovery depends on regime segmentation and a shared event schema. Conformal prediction depends on base predictors. Adversarial verification depends on the typed reasoning state produced by the other predictive and governance signals. Synthetic generation depends on historical regime and failure structure. Active learning depends on uncertainty, disagreement, failure and sparsity signals. Programme synthesis depends on the typed DSL, registry and validation stack. Federated learning is best applied to the representation, routing or specialist-model layers, not to raw trade decisions. The meta-orchestrator depends on almost everything, which is why it should be introduced only after you have reliable health metrics from underlying signals. [R3][R4][R5]

A practical design consequence follows from those dependencies: every signal should write not only its output, but also its **validity horizon**, **freshness**, **regime scope**, **provenance** and **failure code** back to the graph. Without those metadata, the orchestrator cannot tell the difference between a weak signal and a stale signal, and the post-trade loop cannot tell the difference between model error and data error. [R1][R3][R5]

## Fusion architecture

The right fusion design is **dynamic and hierarchical**, not flat. Flat equal-weight fusion ignores that signals operate in different score spaces, different latency budgets and different regimes. A rigid one-direction hierarchy is also too blunt, because it turns one module into a bottleneck or ideology. The best architecture is a three-plane system: an **alpha plane** that generates candidate edge, a **governance plane** that calibrates and challenges that edge, and a **science plane** that continuously proposes, stress-tests and prunes future experts. [R1][R3][R4][R5]

That architecture only works if every signal writes to a canonical typed object rather than a bespoke score. The minimum live schema should look something like: `signal_id`, `as_of`, `instrument`, `horizon`, `edge_mu`, `edge_q05`, `edge_q95`, `regime_id`, `causal_stability`, `freshness`, `evidence_refs`, `compute_cost_ms`, `fail_code`, and `valid_until`. The orchestrator should fuse those typed objects, not raw model logits, raw retrieval ranks or raw prose. [R1][R2][R3][R5]

The orchestration objective should be explicit. Each expert’s influence should be a function of its historical skill in the current regime, its freshness, current coverage health, causal stability, disagreement profile and compute cost. In plain terms: a signal that is historically good but currently stale, miscalibrated or expensive should lose weight; a cheaper signal with stronger live health should gain weight. That is exactly why the architecture should route dynamically by regime, horizon and strategy class rather than allocating fixed weights. [R1][R5]

The hot live stack should therefore be small: Quant ML, regime assignment, conformal wrapping, a lightweight orchestrator, and cached graph/path features. Retrieval, DSLM enrichment and shallow graph lookups belong on the warm path for candidate trades or event windows. Causal discovery, adversarial challenge, synthetic world generation, active-learning selection, federated updates and programme search belong mostly on the cold path and feed the live stack indirectly through updated graph edges, constraints, features and challenger models. [R1][R2][R3][R5]

## Minimum viable subset and build order

If the question is strictly “which five signals deliver roughly 80% of the value for a general quant platform?”, my answer is: **Quant ML models, persistent knowledge graph, conformal prediction, contrastive regime discovery and the meta-orchestrator** — Signals 3, 4, 6, 11 and 13. Together they give you baseline alpha, point-in-time memory and lineage, uncertainty-aware risk control, regime adaptation, and dynamic routing/abstention. That combination addresses the two big realities of live quant trading — uncertainty and non-stationarity — before you pay the heavier compute and governance cost of DSLM and agentic components. [R3][R4][R5]

There is one important caveat. If your proprietary edge is strongly semantic — for example, a distinctive event ontology, rule corpus, market-structure doctrine or text-heavy regime process — then the first augmentation after that five-signal core should be **VectorDB retrieval and the DSLM**. In other words, for a text- and ontology-heavy platform, Signals 1 and 2 move up the queue very quickly; for a more classical systematic platform, they are powerful but not the first 80/20 win. [R1][R2]

The optimal build order is therefore: first, a point-in-time event store, feature store, model registry and a graph-lite provenance layer that will grow into Signal 4; secondly, live Quant ML as the simplest profitable champion; thirdly, conformal wrappers and coverage dashboards; fourthly, contrastive regime discovery and a simple dynamic router; fifthly, the full knowledge graph and time-travelled memory interfaces; sixthly, VectorDB retrieval and then the DSLM as typed feature emitters; seventhly, causal scoring and adversarial verification as challenge functions; eighthly, synthetic scenario generation and programme synthesis on the research plane; ninthly, active-learning scheduling of research actions; and last, federated learning, which is valuable but only once you genuinely have decentralised data silos and the legal, privacy and confidentiality controls to support it. [R2][R3][R4][R5]

The build principle underneath that sequence is simple: ship cheap, auditable edge first; add uncertainty and regime adaptation next; add semantic and scientific complexity only when the supporting lineage, testing and rollback machinery already exists. That principle is also the safest fit with the formal testing, approval, recordkeeping and resilience obligations imposed on in-scope algorithmic trading and financial ICT systems. citeturn11view0turn6view5turn8view0

## Failure management and disagreement policy

The failure modes are predictable, and that is good news because predictable failure modes can be designed around. Retrieval can return semantically similar but regime-wrong analogues. The DSLM can hallucinate, memorise old narratives or misread a novel setting. Quant ML can overfit or go stale. The graph can self-reinforce bad beliefs if it encodes endogenous market impact as exogenous alpha. Causal discovery can infer fragile directionality under hidden confounding. Conformal prediction can silently under-cover under drift. Debate agents can be persuasive-but-wrong. Synthetic generators can produce beautiful nonsense. Active learning can chase noisy edge cases. Federated learning can leak or import negative transfer. Contrastive models can find clusters with no trading value. Programme synthesis can rediscover overfitting at industrial scale. The meta-orchestrator can become the biggest overfit model in the system if it chases recent winners without decay. [R1][R3][R4][R5]

Conflicts between signals should therefore be resolved in passes, not by clever averaging. First, apply **hard vetoes**: stale or missing point-in-time data, broken timestamps, unapproved model versions, market-access or capital-limit breaches, severe conformal coverage breakdown, market-abuse alerts, or missing route health should all force non-execution. Secondly, apply **size discipline**: if expected edge is positive but the conformal interval overlaps zero materially, or if disagreement is high, size down rather than flip. Thirdly, apply **escalation**: if edge is large and the strategy is slow enough, route the case to adversarial verification or human review. Fourthly, apply **fallback**: if the orchestrator or expensive experts are unhealthy, fall back to the simple champion stack — quant model, regime gating, conformal sizing and deterministic risk controls — until the richer path is revalidated. [R1][R4][R5] citeturn11view0turn8view0turn6view6

In production, I would make disagreement itself a first-class feature. The system should log not just “what did we trade?” but “how much did the experts disagree, why did they disagree, and which disagreements were informative?”. Post-trade blame allocation should then attribute regret to components and interactions, not just to the final ensemble: bad retrieval, wrong regime, stale graph edge, miscoverage, router failure, or execution slippage mismatch. Without that attribution, “self-improving” quickly degenerates into “self-justifying”. [R3][R4][R5]

## Non-stationarity and lifecycle governance

The architecture should treat non-stationarity as the default operating condition, not as an exception. That means the system must be designed to **forget** as carefully as it learns. Graph edges need relation-specific decay; regime manifolds need periodic retraining and live assignment drift checks; causal objects need to be local to instrument, horizon and event type; conformal intervals need rolling recalibration; and expert weights need half-lives and retirement rules. A static expert skill table is not good enough in markets that continuously change their microstructure, participant mix and information transmission channels. [R1][R3][R5]

The practical recipe is straightforward. Use regime-local models and regime-local causal graphs. Make every signal output a validity horizon. Maintain live health metrics for rolling IC, calibration, empirical coverage, turnover, slippage stability, disagreement rate and graph-edge decay. Run champion-challenger deployment with canary capital, not all-at-once promotion. Let the orchestrator spawn, demote or retire experts based on observed regime fitness instead of keeping all signals alive forever. Use synthetic scenario generation not to inflate training data, but to probe the exact failure modes that the active-learning and verifier layers identify as weak. And ensure that “learning from history” always means “learning from correctly time-stamped history”. [R3][R4][R5]

Finally, self-improvement has to be governed like an operationally material trading system, not like a research toy. MiFID RTS 6 requires separated testing environments, controlled deployment, annual self-assessment and validation, stress testing, kill functionality, automated surveillance and real-time monitoring; DORA requires documented ICT risk management, digital operational testing, incident processes and third-party risk management for in-scope EU financial entities; SEC Rule 15c3-5 requires automated pre-trade controls and annual review and certification for covered US market access. NeuroFusion should use those obligations as design anchors: every new expert and every route change should be replayable, attributable, testable, controllable and reversible. [R4][R5] citeturn11view0turn6view5turn12view0turn8view0

In short, the best version of NeuroFusion-13 is a **dynamic hierarchical architecture** with a small live core, a rich memory substrate, formal uncertainty and challenge layers, and a cold research plane that continuously generates and prunes hypotheses. That preserves the intellectual ambition of the 13-signal design while keeping it compatible with quant reality: latency budgets, point-in-time integrity, auditability, model risk, and continuously shifting markets.
