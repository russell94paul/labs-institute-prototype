# Module 2, Lesson 4: Reversals

> **Vol 1, Class 4 Notes — MrWitness** (video unavailable; notes only)

A reversal is the fourth and final stage of algorithmic price delivery. It ends the current trend and begins a new one in the opposite direction. Understanding the three types of reversals — and what distinguishes them from a deep retracement — is critical to not getting trapped at the wrong side of a turn.

---

## What Is a True Reversal?

> "Reversal = Market moves in contrarian direction from the current trend. Happens when Market Makers (Men in Black) take a major pool of liquidity and shift intent to the opposite side." — Class 4 Notes

**The defining rule:**

> "A real reversal must take out the origin swing — the swing low/high where the trend began." — Class 4 Notes

This is what separates a reversal from a deep retracement. A retracement corrects within the trend — it never takes out the origin swing. A reversal takes it out, confirming that the algorithmic program has switched sides.

---

## Where Reversals Form

Reversals form at **big pools of liquidity**. Small swing highs/lows produce short-term reversals (micro-structure). Major, sustained reversals form at:

- Weekly highs/lows
- Daily highs/lows
- 1H or session highs/lows (for intraday reversals)

**Best probability condition:** Multiple liquidity pools stacked at the same level — "layered liquidity pools" (multiple highs/lows at the same price). The algorithm sweeps all of them in one move, then reverses strongly.

---

## The Three Reversal Types

### Type 1: Failure Swing (~10% of reversals)

**What it is:** The market fails to reach its liquidity target. Instead of sweeping the expected swing high/low (BSL or SSL), it turns at an intermediate level — typically a rejection block or the CE (50%) of a wick.

**What it looks like:**
- Price appears to be retracing toward its DOL
- It turns before reaching the DOL
- The reversal point is at a rejection block, a week's CE, or a nearby PDA — not at the main liquidity pool

**Why it fools traders:**
This looks identical to a retracement while it is happening. There is no external confirmation that it will fail — only the post-fact rejection at a non-obvious level reveals it was a failure swing.

**How to handle it:**
- Take partials at rejection blocks. Always.
- "No one knows if it will fail or complete." — Class 4 Notes
- If you hold your full position past a rejection block and it fails, you are holding through a type 1 reversal

**Probability: ~10%**

---

### Type 2: Raid on Stops (~80% of reversals)

**What it is:** The most common reversal pattern. The market runs the liquidity (sweeps the highs or lows, collecting all stops), then immediately rejects and reverses.

**What it looks like:**
- Price approaches a major liquidity pool (BSL or SSL)
- A sharp spike takes out the swing high/low — collecting all the buy/sell stops above/below
- Price immediately reverses — often within a few candles
- A large wick forms at the extreme

**Who gets trapped:**
- **Shorts selling resistance** just before the spike: price sweeps above them, triggering their stops (forcing them to buy back at a loss), then reverses lower — now they missed the move
- **Longs buying the breakout** just as price spikes above the level: they get in right at the high, then price reverses, stopping them out

> "Classic 'get in, get out' behavior. Quick sweep, then reversal. Traps traders." — Class 4 Notes

**Entry approach for the raid on stops reversal:**
- Wait for the sweep to complete (price must take the liquidity level clearly)
- Look for the first FVG or rejection block on the retracement lower (within the same or next few candles)
- Enter at that PDA with stop above the wick high

**Probability: ~80%**

This is the primary reversal model — full entry details in [[concepts/entry-models/reversal-raid-on-stops]].

---

### Type 3: Accumulation of Shorts/Longs (~10% of reversals)

**What it is:** Smart money builds a large position at an extreme — near a major liquidity level — while retail traders interpret the sideways action as continuation (a consolidation within the trend).

**The key distinction:**

| Consolidation | Accumulation (Type 3 Reversal) |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Within a range, away from extremes, before the DOL | At extremes — at or near a major liquidity level |
| Followed by expansion in trend direction | Followed by reversal in the opposite direction |
| Both sides of the range still have liquidity outstanding | Smart money is building positions at the liquidity level |

**Why it fools traders:**
Retail sees a range forming and believes it is a continuation pattern (consolidation before the next expansion). Smart money is actually using the range to accumulate a position for the reversal. The longer the range at the extreme, the larger the position being accumulated.

**How to identify it:**
- The range is forming at or near a major weekly/daily liquidity level (BSL or SSL)
- Smart money is "absorbing" retail longs (for bearish accumulation) at the high
- No significant expansion in trend direction has occurred from the range
- Context: HTF FVG or narrative has shifted (see [[concepts/business-logic/ict-narratives]] and [[concepts/business-logic/ict-market-structure]])

**Probability: ~10%**

## Key Rules (From Class 4 Notes)

1. **Always target the origin swing** — a true reversal must reach it. If price does not take out the origin swing, it was a retracement, not a reversal.
2. **Failure swings are more likely under HRLR conditions** — high resistance, choppy, news-week environments increase the probability of a type 1 failure swing.
3. **Always take partials at rejection blocks** — you cannot know in real-time whether you are in a type 1 (failure swing) or a continuation. Taking partials is the rule, not the exception.
4. **Best probability reversals happen at layered liquidity pools** — multiple stacked highs or lows at the same price = higher probability of a clean raid-on-stops reversal.
5. **Accumulation looks like consolidation but is at extremes** — use HTF context (weekly/daily levels) to distinguish.

---

## Reversal vs. Retracement: The Decision Framework

When price is correcting and you are unsure whether it will continue or reverse:

| Question | If Yes → | If No → |
|----------|---------|---------|
| Is price at a major weekly/daily liquidity level? | Higher reversal probability | Likely retracement |
| Did price take out the liquidity with a spike and immediately reject? | Raid on stops reversal (Type 2) | May still be in retracement |
| Is price at or near the origin swing of the trend? | High reversal probability | Not yet at origin; likely retracement |
| Are there multiple stacked liquidity pools at this level? | Highest reversal probability | Lower probability reversal |

**Default position:** Until the origin swing is taken out, assume retracement. Manage accordingly (take partials at rejection blocks, use the "real retracement" checklist from Lesson 3).

## Manual Intervention (Related Context)

Class 3 notes mention a specific delivery pattern under manual intervention:

> "Manual intervention roadmap: price often runs an intraday high first, then targets the Previous Day Low (PDL). If PDL is not taken on the first attempt → it's pending business."

Manual intervention is a form of forced reversal — an externally imposed price delivery rather than an algorithmically natural one. When it occurs, the reversal can be sudden and sharp, with unusual speed. The "strong move in one direction followed immediately by a strong move in the opposite direction" pattern is the signal.
